Temporal Circular Encoding

What the math actually does, what it doesn't, and whether it's enough.

1. The Core Idea — In One Sentence

We append 2 numbers per time scale to any existing embedding vector. Those numbers encode when the message happened. The database doesn't know they're time — it just sees a slightly longer vector. But the math guarantees that two messages close in time get a similarity boost, and two messages far apart get a penalty.

Original embedding: [0.02, -0.13, 0.07, ... 768 numbers total]
Encoded: [0.02, -0.13, 0.07, ... 0.47, -0.88, 0.31, 0.95]
↑ semantic (unchanged) ↑ wall time ↑ turn position

The extra dimensions are sin/cos pairs. cos(a−b) = cos(a)cos(b) + sin(a)sin(b) means the similarity between any two encoded vectors decomposes into semantic + temporal parts exactly.

2. The Math — Why This Works

cos_sim(q₁,q₂) = γ·sem_sim + γ_wall·cos(λ_wall·π·Δt_wall) + γ_turn·cos(λ_turn·π·Δt_turn)

Key properties

γ + γ_wall + γ_turn = 1 always. It's a convex combination. Semantic and temporal contributions split the total similarity budget. Tune β to increase γ_temporal (less semantic weight, more time sensitivity).

Cosine is bounded in [-1, 1]. At Δt=0 (same time), cos=1 → full temporal bonus. At Δt=1/(2λ), cos=0 → neutral. At Δt=1/λ, cos=−1 → maximum penalty.

Single ANN pass. Encode once, search like normal. No re-rank, no second query, no post-processing. Time is in the vector.

Circular encoding is the only approach that ticks all five boxes AND is built. Hilbert curves encode time into a 1D index (different mechanism, same goal). Hyperbolic embeddings capture conversation tree structure (the thing we DON'T do yet — see below). Log-decay was removed because it double-counts time and diverges to infinity.

5. What It Does NOT Do — The Hard Boundaries

❌ Does not capture conversation tree structure

It knows message 3 and message 4 are adjacent turns. It does not know that message 3 replied to message 1 and message 4 is a side thread branching from message 2. Turn position is a line, not a tree. If two messages are at the same turn depth in different branches, they look identical to the encoding even though one is on-topic and the other is a tangent.

Fix: hyperbolic (Poincaré ball) embeddings. Conversation tree root at origin, replies move outward along geodesic, branching captured by hyperbolic volume. Proven in literature (Nickel & Kiela 2017). 1–2 weeks to prototype.

❌ Does not zero out semantic clones at distant times

Two messages with identical meaning (sem_sim = 1.0) on opposite sides of the time window get a score of 2γ − 1. With γ=0.5 (time_heavy): score = 0.0 (neutral). With γ=0.71 (balanced): score = 0.42 (still positive). Time can demote but never fully negate a perfect semantic match.

Why: the decomposition is additive (γ·sem + γ_t·cos), not multiplicative (sem × cos). To fully zero out a perfect semantic match, you'd need the quaternion phase approach (Section 3 in five-approaches-math.md) — but that requires training and the peer review found the training objective doesn't actually produce multiplicative decomposition.

❌ Does not allow per-query temporal tuning

γ and γᵢ are baked at insert time. You can't say "for this query, weight wall time at 80% and ignore turns." Every query gets the same temporal weighting as every insert.

Workaround: maintain multiple indexes (time_heavy, turns_only, balanced) and route queries to the appropriate one. Not elegant, but functional.

❌ Not monotonic — cosine oscillates

Cosine goes negative at Δt = 1/(2λ), but returns to +1 at Δt = 2/λ. Messages at Δt=0 and Δt=2/λ get identical temporal similarity. For λ=0.05, the first echo is at Δt=40 time units — safely beyond practical conversation spans. But strictly monotonic decay (older = always worse) requires a different function.

The fix we killed: log-decay re-rank. It was monotonic but unbounded and double-counted time. Exponential saturation (α·(1−e^−λt)) would give monotonic decay while staying bounded, if needed.

❌ Breaks if timestamps are missing

Every document must have a value for every temporal scale. If some documents have wall time but no turn position, you can't use both scales on those documents — the norm changes and the convex combination breaks. You'd need separate indexes per feature set.

❌ Not benchmarked on real ANN indexes (pgvector HNSW)

All benchmarks use FAISS flat index (exact search). Real pgvector HNSW performance with 2–4 extra dimensions hasn't been measured. The added dimensions might slightly degrade ANN recall at very large scale (>1M vectors) — standard curse-of-dimensionality concern, untested.

6. The Decision: Is This Enough?

The 80% case is handled. If your retrieval problem is "find messages about X near time Y," circular encoding is proven, bounded, single-pass, and delivers +10–15 points precision with zero model changes. You can ship this.

The remaining 20% — what we'd gain by going further

Conversation tree topology"Find the reply chain that led to this message" — branch-aware retrieval1–2 weeksHyperbolic (Poincaré ball)

Monotonic decayStrict recency: older = always worse, no cosine echoes1 dayExponential saturation penalty
Per-query tuning"This query: 80% wall time. Next query: 80% turns."1–2 daysMultiple indexes + routing

My recommendation: Ship circular encoding now — it's the 80% case, mathematically proven, empirically verified, and zero-risk (pure numpy, no training, no infra change). The missing tree topology is the most interesting next step because it unlocks branch-aware retrieval, which is genuinely new capability vs. "messages near in time." But it's a 1–2 week research project, not a bugfix.
